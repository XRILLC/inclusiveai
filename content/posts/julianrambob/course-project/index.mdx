## Project description
### Overview
We decided to create a dataset to fill in gaps in sentiment analysis when detecting sarcasm. 
Sarcasm is a particularly hard tone for humans to detect sometimes, and is a harder task for a machine.

In order to create a dataset for this, we referred to multiple existing English corpora, and used a corpus specifically labaled for sarcasm.

Once a dataset has been compiled, we will compare the ability of logistic regression to predict sarcasm with and without this dataset.

### Novelty of the Project
The project is novel for a variety of reasons. 
 - the creation of a dataset specifically for detecting sarcasm created from a wide range of internet-based sources such as blogs, reddit, and other popular sites.
 The dataset is also as timely as possible, and we have supplemented data from available corpora with data scraped from various sources as needed.
 
 - Our dataset includes expressions of sarcasm found in social media and online forums, providing a better resource for model training. 
 
 - By providing a comparison of the perforance of the logistic regression model with and without the new dataset, by evaluating a logistic regression model's performance both with and without our dataset, we will show the impact of the dataset on the models ability to better detect sarcasm in the process of sentiment analysis.

### Motivation for the project
The motivation for the project is to look for ways to improve the process of sentiment analysis. When it comes to detection of sarcasm, the ability to detect it sometimes even eludes humans. In using corpus resources that focus on the internet, we seek to determine what kind of texts might improve the process and what features are the most important in detection of sarcasm using sentiment analysis.


### Challenges of task
- Finding up-to-date corpora & the need to supplement through web-scraping
  - Many internet corpora on the larger side (Brown, etc) are not up-to-date to the current year. It might not be as relavent as data from the more recent year or two.
- Determining what model to use for sentiment analysis/classification
  - Should we use a model that is better for sentiment analysis?
  - Would it be more indicative of the model's improvement with new data if we use a model that is not as well-regarded for sentiment analysis? 
- Feature Selection
  - sarcasm doesn't necessarily rely on specific syntactic structures, vocab or other obvious features.
  - What do we need to do to ensure that context is taken into account and to identify other features that are common when sarcasm is present.
  - Is there even a way to do this?

## Results


| **Model**                      | **Accuracy** | **Precision** | **Recall** | **F1 Score** |
|---------------------------------|--------------|---------------|------------|--------------|
| **LogisticRegression**      | 63.16%       | 61.06%        | 65.80%     | 63.34%       |
| **LogisticRegression with BERTVectorizer**         | 62.41%       | 61.38%        | 60.10%     | 60.73%       |
| **Pretrained BERT**             | 56.64%       | 62.72%        | 32.87%     | 43.13%       |

When the model was trained without the BERTVectorizer, it achieved an accuracy of 63.16%, precision of 61.06%, recall of 65.80%, and an F1 score of 63.34%. Introducing the BERTVectorizer into the feature union caused a slight reduction in all performance metrics, yielding an accuracy of 62.41%, precision of 61.38%, recall of 60.10%, and an F1 score of 60.73%. This decline suggests that while BERT embeddings are powerful in capturing semantic nuances, their integration into the pipeline may have resulted in redundancy or feature conflicts with other vectorizers, such as TF-IDF and POS-based features.

Interestingly, when a pretrained BERT model was evaluated on this dataset, its performance was significantly worse. Despite BERT's success in large-scale sarcasm detection tasks, it struggled on this smaller, balanced dataset. The accuracy was 56.64%, precision was 62.72%, recall dropped to 32.87%, and the F1 score was 43.13%. This result highlights the challenges of applying pretrained models without fine-tuning, particularly in specialized tasks like sarcasm detection, where contextual cues are likely crucial. The low recall indicates that the model had trouble detecting the sarcastic class, a problem that is exacerbated by the dataset's smaller size, which may not provide sufficient context for pretrained models.

The dataset used for these evaluations was perfectly balanced, meaning that the baseline accuracy was 50%. This balance helps make the evaluation metrics more robust, but it also reveals the complexity of sarcasm detection, which involves nuances beyond just semantic understanding. Pretrained models, such as BERT, are trained for broader language tasks and may not generalize well to niche tasks like sarcasm detection, especially when not fine-tuned on the specific data. This performance gap underscores the need for dataset-specific adaptations to improve the model's accuracy in such specialized tasks or greater examination of features which may be informative for sarcasm detection in small datasets.


## Error analysis

Error analysis on the held-out test data provides deeper insights into the performance disparities observed. For the pretrained BERT sarcasm detector, false negatives (failing to detect sarcasm) were particularly common, as reflected in the low recall of 32.87% for the sarcastic class. These errors might involved sentences in contexts where sarcasm depends on external knowledge or tone, neither of which the model can fully capture from text alone. False positives, on the other hand, occurred when exaggerated language or hyperbole led the model to misclassify non-sarcastic statements as sarcastic.

Similarly, for the feature union-based model, the integration of the BERTVectorizer did not significantly address these challenges and even performed worse overall. False positives often stemmed from lexical patterns misinterpreted by the TF-IDF and POS-based features, while false negatives persisted for subtle sarcasm likely reliant on conversational or situational cues. The redundancy between BERT embeddings and other feature types may have diluted the model's ability to leverage the unique strengths of each feature set, leading to the observed performance decline when BERTVectorizer was included.

To address these challenges, fine-tuning the pretrained BERT model on this specific dataset could significantly improve its ability to capture the nuances of sarcasm in this domain. Expanding the dataset to include more diverse and representative sarcastic examples could also enhance generalization. There is a good chance the dataset purposefully contains very specific types of textual sarcasm whith corresponding non-sarcastic applications. For example, 24 sarcastic sentences in the dataset begin with "Actually" which intuitevely may be a good indicator of sarcasm accross general datasets but this set also contains 27 nonsarcastic examples which begin the same way. Incorporating external knowledge sources or context could help overcome these limitations and improve the model even with a small training set since it would broaden the considered features which could potentially be sarcasm-specific.
