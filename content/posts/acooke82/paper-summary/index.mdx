## Description

In this paper, the authors discuss how developing AI with understandings about how humans physically interact with the world is critical for building beneficial AI. If the human and AI assessments of the physical world are considered to be the same, we can say that there is human-AI alignment. This alignment leads to better design and heightens user experiences. While some senses, such as vision and hearing, are already being using in AI applications, smell remains an underdeveloped area of research. The authors focus on analyzing if human-AI alignment is possible for smell through LLMs for their work as LLMs are becoming more common as a human interface and can learn concepts by grouping relationships in an embedding space. While LLMs have proven themselves possible to be aligned for other senses, smell is trickier because it is difficult for humans to conceptualize, suggesting that it may be difficult for LLMs to build the concepts necessary to make human-like judgements. The authors set out to test how similar the AI and human concepts of smell are by creating "Sniff AI", a system that analyzes how LLMs encode human scent descriptions and if that embedding space is capable of human-like judgements. The system works by giving human study participants two separate tasks, a Scent Description and an Interactive Scent Comparison Description. The study found that the AI achieved a success rate for the first task of 27.5% and a success rate of 37.5% for the second task. The authors also used other LLMs, such as OpenAI GPT-4, to describe how scents smell to see if AI descriptions would differ from the human participant ones. By comparing term frequency wordclouds produced from both descriptions, it was additionaly confirmed that alignment was low. The outcome could be due to the lack of standardized descriptions for scent, something participants noted they struggled with in the post-experiment interviews. This discrepancy could be also be explained by LLMs use of expert terminology, such as "nutty undertones" or "subtly spicy", descriptions unlikely to be used by a standard participant. The moderately more successful secondary task could be due to the participants' comfortability or experience gained by completing task one. In the future, I think there are two specific additions that could lead to more insight on the AI's results. One would be to add participants who are experts on scent, such as perfumers or sommeliers, and see if their human-AI alignment is closer. Alternatively, if the AI is to cater to the general public, adding training data that is more casual in nature and less chemical could lead to an increased success rate as well.                     

## Motivation

I selected this paper while scrolling through the most recent papers available to read on arxiv.org under the Computation and Language section. While the fun title was my original reason for opening it up, I ultimately selected this paper because I found that their study was very thorough, used interesting methods, and delved into concepts I hadn't considered before. We often see articles with titles suggesting AI models are sentient, or gaining consciousness, due to their ability to hold conversations or assist with physical tasks. This paper touches on similar themes of human-AI interaction and AI resourcefulnes, but highlights the differences between AI and human experiences in terms of physical senses. Some senses are already at the forefront of AI development, such as vision, which is fundamental to technologies in self-driving cars, smart glasses, and cameras. However, smell is not typically a sense I would associate with AI, and I was curious to read their research on how and why we should develop it further.
