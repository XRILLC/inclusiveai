## Description

In this paper, the authors begin by discussing why developing AI that understand how humans physically interact with the world is critical for building benefiical AI. While some senses, such as vision and hearing, are already being using in AI applications, smell remains an underdeveloped area of research. The authors focus on using LLMs for their work as they are becoming more common as a human interface and can learn concepts by grouping relationships in an embedding space. While LLMs have proven themselves successful in other areas, smell is trickier to describe by humans without using specialized terms, suggesting that it may be difficult for LLMs to create strong embeddings. To test how similar the LLM and human experience are in terms to conceptualizing smell, the authors create two separate tasks, one human based and one LLM based.    

## Motivation

I selected this paper while scrolling through the most recent papers available to read on arxiv.org under the Computation and Language section. While the fun title was my original reason for opening it up, I ultimately selected this paper because I found that their study was very thorough, used interesting methods, and delved into concepts I hadn't considered before. We often see articles with titles suggesting AI models are sentient, or gaining consciousness, due to their ability to hold conversations or assist with physical tasks. This paper touches on similar themes of human-AI interaction and AI resourcefulnes, but highlights the differences between AI and human experiences in terms of physical senses. Some senses are already at the forefront of AI development, such as vision, which is fundamental to technologies in self-driving cars, smart glasses, and cameras. However, smell is not typically a sense I would associate with AI, and I was curious to read their research on how and why we should develop it further.
