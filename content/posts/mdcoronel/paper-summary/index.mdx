## Description
This paper describes the authors' approach to use adversairal example generation using confusion sets to make Neural Machine Translation more resilient to Automatic Speech Recognition errors. 

## Motivation
This paper was selected due to its relevance to a project I am working on for my internship. We are working with ASR and NMT and my desire was to find how what latest advancement was being made to datasets to make ASR or NMT better from the start. While this paper didn't address that, I still found it interesting and worth reading.

### Experience using the LLM

Using this LLM was not the most difficult. Getting the Jan UI to work was the most complicated part of this experience. It was easy to ask the questions and  the language returned was layman and easy to interrupt as an United States American speaking a Western American Dialect. It was a very basic  analysis though and not much was gleamed that I couldn't just read on my own. I primarily asked fairly straight-forward questions. But I did ask the model various questions about who the paper would be bested suited to, or how this could be implemented in one's daily life. Again, when the questions were non domain specific, like the latter, the response was "I don't know." The prompt responses were factual to the best of my knowledge, I didn't see any kind of mistakes being made. It seems that the questions that could have had more mistakes would be the opionion based questions that the LLM just answered "I don't know to." 



#### Errors and limitations of the LLM 

This model fell short when asked to generate its own thoughts and opinion. In those examples, the model generated an answer of "I don't know". I found this humorous but did find it's limitation there to make this not the most help LLM for me. It's basic language and lack of elaboration might make this a tool better suited for long documents. But for shorter paper summaries. It does the same job I do at a more simplistic level.
