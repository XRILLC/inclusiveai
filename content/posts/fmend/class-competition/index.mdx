## Task summary

_See [the rubric](https://parsertongue.org/courses/snlp-2/assignments/rubrics/class-competition/final/#task-summary)_

The task was to train a model on a dataset of text pairs, separated by the [SNIPPET] delimiter, which were labeled as written by the same author (1) or not (0). We then had to test the model's ability to discern the difference on a testset without labels. One of the challenges with this task is the dataset size. The dataset is not large enough for a model to get a truly good idea of discernment. We were given the option of beefing up the data as we saw fit to improve the model's performance, but I did not go this route. At a length of only about 1600 text pairs (of which I split 90-10 for validation purposes, so really my model was only trained on less than 1500 text pairs), any model would struggle with confident validation on the testset. Some other challenges include dealing with varying text lengths and styles and capturing stylistic markers distinguishing authors. For my approach, I would say that I did use a SotA approach, though I think I would have done better with DeBERTa. Research has shown that transformer-based models can effectively capture nuance between authors, though I think that the dataset size limitation is the BBEG in this assignment.

## Exploratory data analysis

_See [the rubric](https://parsertongue.org/courses/snlp-2/assignments/rubrics/class-competition/final/#exploratory-data-analysis)_

The dataset uses the [SNIPPET] delimiter to separate text pairs with binary labels to indicate whether the separated texts were written by the same author (1) or not (0). The dataset size per class were as follows: Training samples: 1440; Validation samples: 161; Total samples for training: 1601. 

## Approach

_See [the rubric](https://parsertongue.org/courses/snlp-2/assignments/rubrics/class-competition/final/#approach)_

My approach uses a transformer-based model (RoBERTa) for authorship verification. The texts are processed in pairs, tokenized, and fed through the model which learns to identify writing style similarities. I split the training data into training and validation sets (90-10 split) and set up training for 10 epochs using the AdamW optimizer, with early stopping (patience of 3) to prevent overfitting. In my run, the model achieved about 82% accuracy on the validation set on the first epoch and improved to 84% by the fifth, but stopped at 8 epochs due to plateauing, suggesting that it would certainly benefit from further optimization or a larger dataset. The struggles with this is the length of the dataset. Obviously, this is quite small and results would certainly have been better with a beefed up dataset, but in all honesty, I kinda wanted to see how well I could do with just the data provided. I'm quite happy with my score given those constraints, but I also know I could have done better, even inside of those constraints had I given myself enough time.

## Results

_See [the rubric](https://parsertongue.org/courses/snlp-2/assignments/rubrics/class-competition/final/#results)_

The results of the validation showed varying levels of improvement with the best validation accuracy at epoch 5, shown below. Epoch 1 started off strong with a validation accuracy of 80.75%, which dropped in epoch 2, only to grow again in epoch 3: 83.85%. After epoch 5, we did not see improvements, and because of our patience level of 3, we stopped early after epoch 8. Despite this, epoch 5 showed a really good validation of 84.47%, which is much more than our leaderboard score at 60.95%. This most likely indicates that we are overfitting slightly to the training set compared to the testset. Our model is doing better than random chance.

## Error analysis

_See [the rubric](https://parsertongue.org/courses/snlp-2/assignments/rubrics/class-competition/final/#error-analysis)_

My model achieved ~84% validation accuracy, but only ~61% test accuracy. This suggests that our model struggled with new author pairs and that certain patterns in the test set were not well represented in the training set, meaning that our model was overfitting.
