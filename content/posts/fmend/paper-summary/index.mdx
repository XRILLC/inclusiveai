## Description

This paper introduces M-RAG, a framework for enhancing Large Language Models (LLMs) through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).
The study goes over some limits of then-current RAG methods, mainly in that they treat an entire database as one unit, which can cause
noise which then in turn causes issues in retrieval and generation. The intuition is to separate the data into multiple partitions
which are then treated as individual units, allowing the model to focus on relevant data subsets. The hope is to reduce noise and
improve retrieval quality.

In their methodology, they introduce two distinct agents, Agent-S and Agent-R, and train both of those agents for distinct tasks
through the use of LLMs. Agent-S focuses on iteratively selecting partitions once it receives a query, of which it gains a better 
understanding through the use of a reward system, ultimately refining its retrieval capabilities. Agent-R then takes over, refining the
information retrieved from the partition, ensuring that it is relevant, aiming to better improve generation.

M-RAG demonstrated a marked improvement across multiple tasks across seven datasets. Text summarization had an improvement of 11%
against traditional RAG, machine translation had an 8% improvement, and dialogue generation had a 12% improvement. These scores show
an impressive result, though it did come with some limitations, especially in complexity due to relying on the reinforcement learning
of its two agents.



## Motivation

_Why did you select this paper?_

I chose this specific paper because I'm currently working for a company called View, which is aiming to provide ones own data as a
service. This means that one would be able to harness the power of LLMs on their local machine without having to send their data
elsewhere, meaning that it protects the data privacy of the user. Currently, I am working on evaluating their RAG system and also
providing user-friendly metrics so that a user may also view this type of evaluation, and, whether or not they know about NLP, will
be able to understand, at least on a surface level, how well the system is performing on their data.

While I'm not currently working on improving their RAG system per se, it helps to understand new RAG practices for the purposes of 
improving metrics overall. This paper stood out to me, though its application seems a bit too taxing for the purposes of a local
machine which may or may not have the power of a GPU. In any case, it was a good read.
