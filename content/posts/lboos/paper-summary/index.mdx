## Description

The author proves that a neural network with only one hidden layer and no constraint on the number of nodes is sufficient to approximate any continuous function. (In other words, such a neural network is a universal approximator.) The author shows that various types of functions, such as continuous sigmoidal functions, non-vanishing integrable functions, and identity functions on n-dimensional rectangles, can serve as activation functions in the neural network. He does not make any claim about the number of nodes necessary to approximate a particular function to a fixed amount of accuracy, but he speculates that the number is "astronomical" for  most problems. The proofs are elegant and use several well-known theorems from functional analysis and uniform algebras.

#### What are the results and how do they compare with competing approaches?

The author's result is a theorem that states that for any continuous function f from an interval I to [0,1], there exists a network of intervals (i.e., a collection of open intervals) such that the measure of points incorrectly classified by this network is arbitrarily small.

This result is similar to those using Stone-Weierstrass or Tauberian arguments, which are all related to the concept of approximating continuous functions by step functions. However, the author's theorem has a stronger guarantee: it requires that the measure of points incorrectly classified be "small", whereas competing approaches may only require the existence of such a network.

In particular, Stone-Weierstrass and Tauberian arguments typically rely on the fact that any continuous function can be uniformly approximated by polynomials (or other step functions). However, these arguments do not provide a guarantee about the measure of incorrectly classified points. The author's theorem, on the other hand, provides a stronger result: it shows that for any ε > 0, there exists a network that classifies at least 1 - ε of the points correctly.

The author's approach is unique in that it uses a specific type of network (i.e., intervals) and relies on Lusin's theorem to guarantee the correctness of the classification. This makes the result more accessible to readers who are not familiar with Tauberian arguments or Stone-Weierstrass theory.
