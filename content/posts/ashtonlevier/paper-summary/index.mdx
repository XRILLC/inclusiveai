#### Description (LLM)

- This paper highlights the vulnerability of web-scale datasets used to train large language models (LLMs). It introduces two novel poisoning attacks that can introduce malicious examples into these datasets, potentially compromising the integrity and reliability of the trained models.

The first attack, split-view poisoning, exploits the mutable nature of internet content to ensure that the dataset annotators see different content than the clients who download the dataset later. This allows attackers to inject malicious examples that will be included in the training data.

The second attack, frontrunning poisoning, targets web-scale datasets that periodically snapshot crowd-sourced content, such as Wikipedia.

#### What are the authors proposing?
- The authors propose two novel poisoning attacks that exploit the mutable nature of web-scale datasets to introduce malicious examples:  

Split-view poisoning: This attack takes advantage of the fact that dataset annotators and subsequent users may see different versions of the same content. By manipulating the content before it's indexed and after, attackers can introduce malicious examples that will be included in the training data.  

Frontrunning poisoning: This attack targets datasets that periodically snapshot crowd-sourced content, like Wikipedia. Attackers can inject malicious examples within a limited time window before the snapshot is taken, ensuring that the malicious content is included in the training data.  

The authors demonstrate the feasibility of these attacks on several popular web-scale datasets, highlighting the need for robust defenses to protect against dataset poisoning.

They also propose several low-overhead defenses, such as cryptographic integrity checks and randomized crawling, to mitigate these attacks.

#### What is the approach or innovation?

##### Prompt
- The authors' approach is novel in that it targets the fundamental assumptions about the integrity of web-scale datasets used to train large language models. They identify two key vulnerabilities:

Mutable nature of internet content: They exploit the fact that web content can change over time, leading to discrepancies between what annotators see and what subsequent users download. This allows attackers to inject malicious examples that will be included in the training data.

Periodic snapshots of crowd-sourced content: They target datasets that periodically snapshot content from sources like Wikipedia. By injecting malicious examples within a limited time window before the snapshot, attackers can ensure that the malicious content is included in the training data.

This approach is novel because it challenges the traditional assumption that web-scale datasets are static and reliable. By demonstrating the feasibility of these attacks, the authors highlight the need for robust security measures to protect the integrity of these datasets.

#### What are the results and how do they compare with competing approaches?

##### Prompt
- The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their proposed attacks on several popular web-scale datasets, including LAION-400M and COYO-700M. They estimate that a low-resourced attacker could poison 0.01% of these datasets for just $60.  

This research is novel compared to previous work on poisoning attacks, which often focuses on targeted attacks against specific models or datasets. The authors' approach targets the fundamental vulnerabilities of web-scale datasets themselves, making it more broadly applicable and impactful.  

To mitigate these attacks, the authors propose two low-overhead defenses:

Integrity verification: This defense involves distributing cryptographic hashes for all indexed content, ensuring that clients observe the same data as when maintainers first indexed and annotated it.  

Randomized crawling: This defense involves randomly selecting content to crawl, making it more difficult for attackers to target specific pages or time windows for injection.

While these defenses can help mitigate the risks of poisoning attacks, they are not foolproof. The authors emphasize the need for ongoing research and development to address the evolving landscape of dataset poisoning threats.
