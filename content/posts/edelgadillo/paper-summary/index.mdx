## Description

This paper compares the diversity of responses from 8 LLM models (2 closed models and 6 open models) to responses from people collected on the X app(formerly twitter). 
The authors' goal is to highlight one of the drawbacks of LLM responses to open ended opinionated questions and how it could negatively impact the world from an anthropological perspective
and provide potential easy solutions to help ensure diversity of LLM responses. For their research, they asked each model the same 3 questions, 10 times and for the X responses, they took 
the first 10 responses for each question. They had a total of 6 rounds of testing for the open models; a baseline round, increasing the temperature to 1, changing the prompt two different 
ways, and then combining each of the prompt changes with the temperature increase. For the closed models, they were only able to perform the 2 prompt changes. Their results indicated that 
the baseline diversity was indeed much lower for the LLM's compared to the human responses, with an average of about 26 different answers per question whereas the LLMs had a baseline average
of only 10 different answers per question. They also found that for open models having a higher temperature of 1 and updating the prompt vastly improved the diversity in the LLM responses. 
For the closed models, they found that updating the prompt on its own still made a significant difference in the number of different responses provided by the LLMs.

#### What are the authors proposing?

The authors propose a technique called "tail growth", where they add multiple alternative heads to the existing output head of a large language model (LLM). These additional heads, referred to as "tails", represent different possible responses to an input.
During training, the LLM is forced to learn how to produce outputs for each of these tails. This helps the model to learn a more diverse range of possible responses and improve its ability to generate outputs that are similar in style or tone, but differ in content.
The idea behind tail growth is that by allowing the model to explore different "tails", it can develop a greater understanding of the nuances and complexities of language, and produce more varied and creative outputs. The authors propose using techniques such as adversarial training, where one head of the model is trained to produce the "correct" output, while another head is trained to produce an alternative output that is similar in style or tone but different in content.
The authors claim that this approach can lead to increased diversity in the output of large language models, and improve their ability to generate outputs that are more similar to human-written text.

Once I reviewed the baseline answer, I determined if I needed more or less detail in the response. For this particular set of questions, I most often needed less details to avoid overly long responses.

#### Errors and limitations of the LLM 

There are always a few frustrating moments when trying to get an LLM to do something specific. The first problem I ran into was when I asked it to shorten one of the responses. I had asked the same question multiple times but with varying forms of "succinct" and "short" but the responses became too short and would not included more details for a longer response no matter what I asked.
I ended up having to start 13 conversations due to the LLM holding onto previous context that was causing issues with the responses. It was unfortunate because I liked how it built off of responses to previous questions, but I unfortunately had to sacrifice that in order for certain responses to be short enough but contain enough details. 
Another thing the LLM struggled with was, some of the longer responses contained more fleshed out data points with percentages that it lost in the shorter responses even though the prompt asked it to provide data to help with understanding. It was hit or miss rather concrete data points were included in the responses. 
With this particular model, I was also unable to upload a pdf of the document for it to read, I could only provide the name of the paper with the authors in the prompt. This did not work every time; I ended up recieving responses that started with, "I couldn't find specific information on the paper...".
