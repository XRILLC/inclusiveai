## Description

This paper explores the internal process of Large Language Model (LLM) on interpreting typoglycemia which is a human psychology phenomenon. Typoglycemia refers to a phenomenon where humans can process words or sentences when the word has wrong segment order while preserving the first and last characters. As research regarding investigating the internal
mechanism of LLM showed significant promise in addressing complex tasks, the study tried to reveal the internal mechanism of different LLMs using typoglycemia tasks.
The main research question of the paper is whether the LLM could posses human-like cognitive processes and mechnisms in reading and comprehending. 
The study used 8 different LLMs on 5 datasets to test whether they possess similar mechanism when processing typoglycemia texts in three different levels:character, word and sentence.
The overall results found that these LLMs exhibited human-like behvior in processing the scrambled texts with a higher computational cost.
However, the human-like behavior of LLMs are based on statistical and data-driven rather than genuinely resembing human cognition. 
Among several models, GPT-4 had the highest accracy in reading the scrambled text, and each model showed its unique and consistent hidden layer semantic distribution across different
typoglycemia tasks, reflecting how individual humans possess their own cognitive pattern.

## Motivation

_Why did you select this paper?_
The motivation of selecting this paper is based on my interest in dealing with Korean typoglycemia phenomenon. As my course project, I would like to
fine-tune pre-trained language model with scrambled Korean texts to improve its performance on translating scrambled Korean text into English.
While searching for related studies, I found this paper would provide useful information in conducting my own research project. Although the typoglycemia between
English and Korean is slightly different, this paper provided several insights that I can think of while doing my own course project.
